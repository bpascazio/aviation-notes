# Airworthiness Requirements - PPL Practical Exam Oral

## Overview

Airworthiness requirements ensure that aircraft are maintained in a safe condition for flight. As a Private Pilot License (PPL) holder, you must understand required documents, inspections, Airworthiness Directives (ADs), and maintenance requirements for EASA, FAA, and Thailand CAAT regulations.

|-------------|------|-----|----------------|
| **Airworthiness Certificate** | ✅ Required (Certificate of Airworthiness) | ✅ Required (displayed in aircraft) | ✅ Required (Certificate of Airworthiness) |
| **Registration Certificate** | ✅ Required onboard | ✅ Required onboard (original or copy) | ✅ Required onboard |

| **Technical Log/Journey Log** | ✅ Required (varies by operation) | ⚠️ Not required onboard (must be available) | ✅ Required (varies by operation) |
| **Radio License** | ✅ Required if using radio | ✅ Required if using radio (FCC) | ✅ Required if using radio (NBTC) |

## Required Documents

### EASA Requirements

#### Certificate of Airworthiness (C of A)
- **Purpose**: Certifies that the aircraft meets design standards and is safe for flight
- **Validity**: Remains valid as long as:
  - Aircraft is maintained in accordance with approved maintenance program
  - All applicable ADs are complied with
  - Required inspections are performed
- **Location**: Must be carried onboard the aircraft
- **Issued by**: National aviation authority (EASA member state)
- **Types**: 
  - Standard C of A (normal category)
  - Restricted C of A (special purpose)
  - Permit to Fly (experimental, vintage)

#### Certificate of Registration
- **Purpose**: Proof of aircraft ownership and registration
- **Validity**: Remains valid until aircraft is sold or registration is cancelled
- **Location**: Must be carried onboard
- **Contains**: Aircraft registration mark, owner information, aircraft details

#### Noise Certificate
- **Purpose**: Certifies aircraft meets noise standards (ICAO Annex 16)
- **Required**: For all aircraft operating in EASA member states
- **Validity**: Permanent (unless aircraft is modified)
- **Location**: Must be carried onboard

#### Aircraft Flight Manual (AFM)
- **Purpose**: Contains operating limitations, procedures, and performance data
- **Required**: Must be complete and current with all supplements
- **Location**: Must be available to pilot (can be electronic)
- **Contents**: 
  - Operating limitations
  - Normal procedures
  - Emergency procedures
  - Performance data
  - Weight and balance information

#### Aircraft Technical Log (ATL) or Journey Log
- **Purpose**: Records flight time, maintenance entries, and aircraft status
- **Required**: Varies by operation type and national requirements
- **Location**: Must be carried onboard (when required)
- **Contents**:
  - Flight time and cycles
  - Maintenance entries
  - Defect reports
  - AD compliance records
  - Aircraft status (serviceable/unserviceable)
- **When Required**: 
  - Often required for commercial operations (Part-CAT)
  - May be required for certain aircraft types
  - Check national AIP for specific requirements
- **Maintenance**: Must be kept current and up-to-date
- **Purpose**: Provides continuous record of aircraft airworthiness status

#### Radio Station License
- **Purpose**: Authorization to operate radio equipment on the aircraft
- **Required**: If aircraft radio equipment is used
- **Issued by**: National telecommunications authority (varies by country)
- **Validity**: Typically valid for several years, check expiration date
- **Location**: Must be carried onboard
- **Contains**: 
  - Aircraft registration
  - Radio equipment details
  - License number
  - Validity period
- **Note**: Required even for VFR flights if using radio for communication
- **Compliance**: Operating radio without valid license may result in penalties

### FAA Requirements

#### Airworthiness Certificate
- **Purpose**: Certifies aircraft conforms to type design and is in condition for safe operation
- **Validity**: Remains valid as long as:
  - Aircraft is maintained in accordance with FAR Part 43
  - All applicable ADs are complied with
  - Required inspections are performed
- **Location**: Must be **displayed** in the aircraft (usually near cockpit entrance)
- **Types**:
  - Standard Airworthiness Certificate (normal, utility, acrobatic, commuter, transport)
  - Special Airworthiness Certificate (experimental, limited, restricted, provisional)

#### Registration Certificate
- **Purpose**: Proof of aircraft ownership and registration
- **Validity**: Remains valid until aircraft is sold or registration is cancelled
- **Location**: Must be onboard (original or copy acceptable)
- **Contains**: N-number, owner information, aircraft details

#### Aircraft Flight Manual (AFM) or Pilot's Operating Handbook (POH)
- **Purpose**: Contains operating limitations and procedures
- **Required**: Must be complete and current
- **Location**: Must be available to pilot
- **Contents**: 
  - Operating limitations (FAR 91.9)
  - Normal procedures
  - Emergency procedures
  - Performance data
  - Weight and balance information

#### Journey Log (Not Required Onboard)
- **Purpose**: Records flight time and basic flight information
- **Required**: Not required to be carried onboard, but must be maintained
- **Location**: Must be available for inspection
- **Contents**:
  - Flight time
  - Date of flight
  - Departure and destination
  - Pilot name
  - Basic flight information
- **Note**: Different from maintenance records - this is a flight log
- **FAA**: Not required to be onboard, but must be available

#### Radio Station License (FCC)
- **Purpose**: Authorization to operate radio equipment on the aircraft
- **Required**: If operating radio equipment (FAR 91.203)
- **Issued by**: Federal Communications Commission (FCC)
- **Validity**: Typically valid for 10 years, check expiration date
- **Location**: Must be carried onboard
- **Contains**: 
  - Aircraft registration (N-number)
  - Radio equipment details
  - FCC license number
  - Validity period
- **Note**: Required for all aircraft using radio equipment, including VFR flights
- **Compliance**: Operating radio without valid FCC license violates FCC regulations
- **How to Obtain**: Apply through FCC website or mail
- **Renewal**: Must be renewed before expiration

## Inspections

### EASA Inspections

#### Annual Inspection
- **Frequency**: Every 12 months
- **Purpose**: Comprehensive inspection to ensure continued airworthiness
- **Performed by**: Licensed Aircraft Maintenance Engineer (LAME) or Approved Maintenance Organization (AMO)
- **Scope**: 
  - Complete inspection of aircraft structure
  - Engine inspection
  - Systems inspection
  - Compliance with ADs
  - Review of maintenance records
- **Documentation**: Entry in aircraft maintenance records
- **Validity**: Aircraft cannot fly if annual inspection is expired

#### Preflight Inspection
- **Frequency**: Before each flight
- **Purpose**: Verify aircraft is safe for flight
- **Performed by**: Pilot in command
- **Scope**: 
  - Visual inspection of aircraft exterior
  - Check of fuel, oil, and other fluids
  - Control surface checks
  - Landing gear inspection
  - Documentation check
- **Documentation**: Not required to be logged, but should be performed

#### Additional Inspections
- **100-Hour Inspection**: Not required by EASA (FAA requirement)
- **Progressive Inspections**: May be used as alternative to annual inspection
- **Special Inspections**: Required after certain events (hard landing, lightning strike, etc.)

### FAA Inspections

#### Annual Inspection
- **Frequency**: Every 12 **calendar months** (not flight hours)
- **Purpose**: Comprehensive inspection to ensure continued airworthiness
- **Performed by**: Certified A&P (Airframe and Powerplant) mechanic with Inspection Authorization (IA)
- **Scope**: 
  - Complete inspection per FAR Part 43 Appendix D
  - Engine inspection
  - Systems inspection
  - Compliance with ADs
  - Review of maintenance records
- **Documentation**: Entry in aircraft maintenance records (FAR Part 91.417)
- **Validity**: Aircraft cannot fly if annual inspection is expired
- **Note**: If annual expires, aircraft is grounded until inspection is completed

#### 100-Hour Inspection
- **Frequency**: Every 100 flight hours
- **Purpose**: Similar to annual but required for aircraft used for hire or flight training
- **Performed by**: Certified A&P mechanic
- **Scope**: Same as annual inspection
- **Documentation**: Entry in aircraft maintenance records
- **Validity**: 
  - Can exceed 100 hours by up to 10 hours for positioning to inspection facility
  - Not required for aircraft not used for hire
- **Note**: Does not replace annual inspection

#### Preflight Inspection
- **Frequency**: Before each flight
- **Purpose**: Verify aircraft is safe for flight
- **Performed by**: Pilot in command (FAR 91.3)
- **Scope**: 
  - Visual inspection of aircraft exterior
  - Check of fuel, oil, and other fluids
  - Control surface checks
  - Landing gear inspection
  - Documentation check
- **Documentation**: Not required to be logged, but must be performed

#### Progressive Inspection
- **Alternative**: Can be used instead of annual inspection
- **Purpose**: Spread inspection work over time
- **Requirements**: Must be approved by FAA
- **Frequency**: Based on approved program

### Thailand CAAT Inspections

#### Annual Inspection
- **Frequency**: Every 12 months
- **Purpose**: Comprehensive inspection to ensure continued airworthiness
- **Performed by**: Licensed Aircraft Maintenance Engineer (LAME) or Approved Maintenance Organization (AMO) approved by CAAT
- **Scope**: 
  - Complete inspection of aircraft structure
  - Engine inspection
  - Systems inspection
  - Compliance with ADs
  - Review of maintenance records
- **Documentation**: Entry in aircraft maintenance records
- **Validity**: Aircraft cannot fly if annual inspection is expired
- **Standards**: Follows ICAO standards and CAAT regulations

#### Preflight Inspection
- **Frequency**: Before each flight
- **Purpose**: Verify aircraft is safe for flight
- **Performed by**: Pilot in command
- **Scope**: 
  - Visual inspection of aircraft exterior
  - Check of fuel, oil, and other fluids
  - Control surface checks
  - Landing gear inspection
  - Documentation check
- **Documentation**: Not required to be logged, but should be performed

#### Additional Inspections
- **100-Hour Inspection**: May be required for certain operations (check CAAT regulations)
- **Progressive Inspections**: May be used as alternative to annual inspection (if approved)
- **Special Inspections**: Required after certain events (hard landing, lightning strike, etc.)
- **Periodic Inspections**: May be required based on aircraft type and operation

## Airworthiness Directives (ADs)

### EASA Airworthiness Directives

#### What are ADs?
- **Definition**: Mandatory instructions issued by EASA or national aviation authority
- **Purpose**: Correct unsafe conditions in aircraft, engines, propellers, or appliances
- **Legal Status**: **Mandatory compliance** - cannot be ignored
- **Types**:
  - **Emergency ADs**: Immediate action required
  - **Normal ADs**: Compliance within specified time
  - **One-time ADs**: Single compliance action
  - **Recurring ADs**: Periodic compliance required

#### AD Compliance
- **Responsibility**: Aircraft owner/operator
- **Verification**: Checked during annual inspection
- **Documentation**: Must be recorded in maintenance records
- **Non-compliance**: Aircraft is not airworthy if AD is not complied with
- **How to Check**: 
  - EASA AD database
  - National aviation authority publications
  - Maintenance facility records
  - Aircraft maintenance records

#### AD Categories
- **Category 1**: Immediate compliance required (ground aircraft until complied)
- **Category 2**: Compliance within specified time (may continue flying)
- **Category 3**: Compliance at next inspection or within time limit

### FAA Airworthiness Directives

#### What are ADs?
- **Definition**: Regulations issued by FAA to correct unsafe conditions
- **Purpose**: Address safety issues in aircraft, engines, propellers, or appliances
- **Legal Status**: **Mandatory compliance** - federal regulation (FAR Part 39)
- **Types**:
  - **Emergency ADs**: Immediate action required
  - **Normal ADs**: Compliance within specified time
  - **One-time ADs**: Single compliance action
  - **Recurring ADs**: Periodic compliance required (e.g., every 100 hours)

#### AD Compliance
- **Responsibility**: Aircraft owner/operator
- **Verification**: Checked during annual and 100-hour inspections
- **Documentation**: Must be recorded in maintenance records (FAR 91.417)
- **Non-compliance**: Aircraft is not airworthy - cannot fly legally
- **How to Check**: 
  - FAA AD database
  - Aircraft maintenance records
  - Maintenance facility
  - FAA website

#### AD Format
- **AD Number**: e.g., AD 2023-15-12
- **Applicability**: Which aircraft/engines/appliances affected
- **Compliance Time**: When compliance is required
- **Compliance Method**: How to comply (inspection, modification, replacement)
- **Alternative Methods**: May be approved by FAA

#### AD Compliance Times
- **Immediate**: Ground aircraft until complied
- **Hours**: Within specified flight hours
- **Calendar Time**: Within specified days/months
- **Next Inspection**: At next annual or 100-hour inspection
- **Recurring**: Periodic compliance (e.g., every 100 hours, every annual)

### Thailand CAAT Airworthiness Directives

#### What are ADs?
- **Definition**: Mandatory instructions issued by CAAT or adopted from ICAO/EASA/FAA
- **Purpose**: Correct unsafe conditions in aircraft, engines, propellers, or appliances
- **Legal Status**: **Mandatory compliance** - cannot be ignored
- **Types**:
  - **Emergency ADs**: Immediate action required
  - **Normal ADs**: Compliance within specified time
  - **One-time ADs**: Single compliance action
  - **Recurring ADs**: Periodic compliance required
- **Source**: CAAT may issue its own ADs or adopt ADs from other authorities (EASA, FAA)

#### AD Compliance
- **Responsibility**: Aircraft owner/operator
- **Verification**: Checked during annual inspection
- **Documentation**: Must be recorded in maintenance records
- **Non-compliance**: Aircraft is not airworthy if AD is not complied with
- **How to Check**: 
  - CAAT AD database
  - CAAT publications and notices
  - Maintenance facility records
  - Aircraft maintenance records
  - ICAO AD information
- **International ADs**: CAAT may adopt ADs from EASA, FAA, or other authorities

#### AD Categories
- **Category 1**: Immediate compliance required (ground aircraft until complied)
- **Category 2**: Compliance within specified time (may continue flying)
- **Category 3**: Compliance at next inspection or within time limit

## Maintenance Requirements

### EASA Maintenance Requirements

#### Maintenance Program
- **Requirement**: Aircraft must be maintained per approved maintenance program
- **Types**:
  - Manufacturer's maintenance program
  - Approved maintenance schedule
  - Custom maintenance program (approved by authority)
- **Documentation**: Maintenance records must be kept

#### Maintenance Records
- **Requirement**: Must be maintained and available
- **Contents**:
  - Maintenance performed
  - Inspections completed
  - AD compliance
  - Modifications and repairs
  - Component replacements
- **Retention**: Must be kept for life of aircraft
- **Location**: Not required onboard, but must be available

#### Who Can Perform Maintenance
- **Licensed Aircraft Maintenance Engineer (LAME)**: Can perform maintenance and inspections
- **Approved Maintenance Organization (AMO)**: Can perform maintenance
- **Owner/Operator**: Limited maintenance allowed (preventive maintenance)
- **Certification**: Maintenance must be certified by authorized person

#### Preventive Maintenance (Owner/Operator)
- **Allowed Tasks**: Limited tasks that owner can perform
- **Examples**: 
  - Replacing batteries
  - Replacing landing light bulbs
  - Lubrication
  - Simple inspections
- **Documentation**: Must be recorded in maintenance records
- **Limitations**: Cannot perform major repairs or modifications

### FAA Maintenance Requirements

#### Maintenance Program
- **Requirement**: Aircraft must be maintained per FAR Part 43
- **Types**:
  - Manufacturer's maintenance manual
  - Approved maintenance schedule
- **Documentation**: Maintenance records required (FAR Part 91.417)

#### Maintenance Records (FAR Part 91.417)
- **Requirement**: Must be maintained and available
- **Contents**:
  - Description of work performed
  - Date of work
  - Aircraft total time
  - Signature and certificate number of person performing work
  - AD compliance records
- **Retention**: Must be kept for life of aircraft
- **Location**: Not required onboard, but must be available for inspection

#### Who Can Perform Maintenance
- **A&P Mechanic**: Can perform maintenance and inspections
- **IA (Inspection Authorization)**: Can perform annual inspections
- **Repair Station**: Can perform maintenance per their rating
- **Owner/Operator**: Can perform preventive maintenance (FAR Part 43 Appendix A)

#### Preventive Maintenance (FAR Part 43 Appendix A)
- **Allowed Tasks**: Specific tasks owner can perform
- **Examples**:
  - Replacing batteries
  - Replacing landing light bulbs
  - Lubrication
  - Replacing safety belts
  - Replacing seats
  - Simple inspections
- **Documentation**: Must be recorded in maintenance records
- **Limitations**: 
  - Cannot perform major repairs
  - Cannot perform major alterations
  - Cannot perform annual inspections
  - Cannot perform 100-hour inspections

#### Major vs Minor
- **Major Repair/Alteration**: Requires FAA Form 337 and certification
- **Minor Repair/Alteration**: Can be logged in maintenance records
- **Determination**: Based on FAR Part 43 definitions

### Thailand CAAT Maintenance Requirements

#### Maintenance Program
- **Requirement**: Aircraft must be maintained per approved maintenance program
- **Types**:
  - Manufacturer's maintenance program
  - Approved maintenance schedule
  - Custom maintenance program (approved by CAAT)
- **Documentation**: Maintenance records must be kept
- **Standards**: Follows ICAO standards (Annex 8) and CAAT regulations

#### Maintenance Records
- **Requirement**: Must be maintained and available
- **Contents**:
  - Maintenance performed
  - Inspections completed
  - AD compliance
  - Modifications and repairs
  - Component replacements
- **Retention**: Must be kept for life of aircraft
- **Location**: Not required onboard, but must be available
- **Language**: May be in Thai or English (check CAAT requirements)

#### Who Can Perform Maintenance
- **Licensed Aircraft Maintenance Engineer (LAME)**: Can perform maintenance and inspections (licensed by CAAT)
- **Approved Maintenance Organization (AMO)**: Can perform maintenance (approved by CAAT)
- **Owner/Operator**: Limited maintenance allowed (preventive maintenance)
- **Certification**: Maintenance must be certified by authorized person
- **Standards**: Must meet CAAT and ICAO standards

#### Preventive Maintenance (Owner/Operator)
- **Allowed Tasks**: Limited tasks that owner can perform (as specified by CAAT)
- **Examples**: 
  - Replacing batteries
  - Replacing landing light bulbs
  - Lubrication
  - Simple inspections
- **Documentation**: Must be recorded in maintenance records
- **Limitations**: Cannot perform major repairs or modifications
- **Regulation**: Check CAAT regulations for specific allowed tasks

## Preflight Airworthiness Check

### What to Check Before Flight

#### Documents
- ✅ Airworthiness Certificate (valid and displayed/onboard)
- ✅ Registration Certificate (current)
- ✅ Flight Manual/POH (current and complete)
- ✅ Weight and Balance Data (current)
- ✅ Radio Station License (current, if using radio)
- ✅ Journey Log/Technical Log (current, if required)

#### Maintenance Status
- ✅ Annual Inspection (current - within 12 months)
- ✅ 100-Hour Inspection (current if required)
- ✅ AD Compliance (all applicable ADs complied with)
- ✅ Maintenance Records (available for review)

#### Physical Inspection
- ✅ Preflight inspection completed
- ✅ No obvious damage or defects
- ✅ All required equipment functional
- ✅ ✅ No unapproved modifications

## Common Oral Exam Questions

### Required Documents
- **Q**: What documents must be onboard the aircraft?
- **A**: Airworthiness Certificate, Registration Certificate, Flight Manual/POH, Weight and Balance Data, Radio Station License (if using radio), and Journey Log/Technical Log (if required by operation type)

### Annual Inspection
- **Q**: How often is an annual inspection required?
- **A**: Every 12 calendar months (FAA) or 12 months (EASA)

### 100-Hour Inspection
- **Q**: When is a 100-hour inspection required?
- **A**: For aircraft used for hire or flight training (FAA only)

### Airworthiness Directives
- **Q**: What is an AD and must you comply?
- **A**: Mandatory safety instructions - yes, compliance is required for aircraft to be airworthy

### AD Compliance
- **Q**: How do you know if your aircraft has complied with all ADs?
- **A**: Check maintenance records, which should document all AD compliance

### Maintenance Records
- **Q**: Where must maintenance records be kept?
- **A**: Not required onboard, but must be available for inspection

### Radio License
- **Q**: Do you need a radio license to use the radio?
- **A**: Yes, if the aircraft has radio equipment, you need a valid Radio Station License (FCC for FAA, national authority for EASA)
- **Q**: What happens if your radio license expires?
- **A**: You cannot legally operate the radio equipment until the license is renewed

### Journey Log/Technical Log
- **Q**: Is a journey log required onboard?
- **A**: For EASA, it depends on operation type and national requirements. For FAA, it's not required onboard but must be maintained and available. For Thailand CAAT, it depends on operation type - check CAAT regulations for specific requirements.

### Preventive Maintenance
- **Q**: What maintenance can an owner perform?
- **A**: Limited preventive maintenance tasks as specified in regulations (FAR Part 43 Appendix A for FAA)

### Expired Inspection
- **Q**: Can you fly if the annual inspection has expired?
- **A**: No - aircraft is not airworthy and cannot be flown legally

### Thailand CAAT Specific Questions
- **Q**: Who issues the Certificate of Airworthiness in Thailand?
- **A**: Civil Aviation Authority of Thailand (CAAT)
- **Q**: What is the aircraft registration format for Thai-registered aircraft?
- **A**: HS-### (e.g., HS-ABC)
- **Q**: Who issues radio licenses in Thailand?
- **A**: National Broadcasting and Telecommunications Commission (NBTC)
- **Q**: Does Thailand follow ICAO standards?
- **A**: Yes, Thailand follows ICAO standards (Annex 8 for airworthiness)
- **Q**: Can CAAT adopt ADs from other authorities?
- **A**: Yes, CAAT may adopt ADs from EASA, FAA, or other aviation authorities

## Important Notes

- **Airworthiness is Required**: Aircraft must be airworthy before every flight
- **Pilot Responsibility**: Pilot in command is responsible for determining aircraft is airworthy
- **Documentation**: All required documents must be current and available
- **AD Compliance**: Non-compliance with ADs makes aircraft unairworthy
- **Inspections**: Expired inspections ground the aircraft
- **Maintenance Records**: Must be available for inspection by authorities
- **Local Variations**: Some EASA member states may have additional requirements
- **Thailand**: Follows ICAO standards with CAAT-specific regulations
